## Description

The Health and Retirement Study (HRS) is a longitudinal survey of individuals close to retirement age. Questions cover a wide range of topics related to health, wealth, employment, insurance, and family structure. The sample size is approximately 7,500 individuals in the original cohort, with smaller cohorts added later. The longitudinal design makes it possible to examine changes in individuals over time, making it especially powerful for certain research questions.

The first wave of respondents was originally surveyed in 1992 and again every two years thereafter. This group consisted of individuals between the ages of 51 and 61. In 1993, an additional cohort of individuals over the age of 70 was added — also referred to as the Asset and Health Dynamics Among the Oldest Old (AHEAD) study. In 1998, the Children of the Depression (CODA) and War Baby (WB) cohorts were added, each consisting of approximately 2,500 respondents.

The HRS contains detailed information on health and wealth. Health questions address heart disease, cancer, lung disease, stroke, and diabetes, as well as respondents' ability to engage in activities of daily living. The wealth section covers income from various sources, savings, home values, and retirement accounts.

The HRS also includes a section on expectations: starting in the first wave, respondents were asked about the likelihood of certain future events (e.g., living to age 65, loaning money to a family member), enabling comparisons with later-wave outcomes.

The RAND Corporation has produced user-friendly versions of the HRS data with renamed variables consistent across years and imputed wealth, income, and medical expenditures. Researchers should be aware that some inconsistencies in these variables remain, even in the RAND data.

## Access

All waves of the HRS are free and publicly available on the [HRS website](http://hrsonline.isr.umich.edu/). However, certain identifying information — including state, county, and zip code of residence; industry and occupation codes; and links to Medicare claims records — is excluded from the public dataset. These restricted fields can be obtained under restricted-use agreements, which require justification for use of the restricted fields and a data-protection plan ensuring data security.
